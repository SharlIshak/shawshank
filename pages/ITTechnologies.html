    1. DATA SCIENCE

    What does it do?

    Data scientists work with data to obtain information and derive insights for businesses. Tasks include capturing, maintaining, processing and communicating data. Most data scientists have training and education in programming, statistics, mathematics and data visualisation. Furthermore, they are also proficient in technologies like Apache Hadoop, GitHub, Tableau and MapReduce. In 2018, it was estimated that 80% of global data was unstructured, meaning that it is not modelled or organised and is often “text-heavy”. Data scientists extract value from large amounts of unstructured information and businesses use this newly organised information to make better decisions based on quantifiable evidence. Data can be used to compare historical trends and analyse competitors and the market. This can help businesses respond to market trends and promote their products or services in areas where they will sell best. Information gleaned from employment websites, social media and business databases is used to better manage employee recruitment. Companies use this data to sort applicants and ensure potential employees suit the organisation’s ethos. Whilst data science has already proven to be an invaluable resource for businesses, future developments are just as exciting. In 2019, improved technologies and refined methods of data science has led to its application in conservative and highly regulated fields like healthcare. In a challenge carried out by the reputable online data science community, Kaggle, teams were asked to develop a data science model to classify chest and lung issues. In conjunction with organisations in the field, data science was used to identify disease and culminated in US FDA clearance of the first articifical intelligence algorithms in X-Rays to automatically scan for disease instead of waiting for a radiologist to review. A further use in the healthcare field includes the identification of drug-supplemental interactions through Supp.ai developed by the Allen Institute for Artificial Intelligence (AI2). This website automatically extracts evidence of supplement and drug interactions for scientific literature and presents them to user.

    Data science impacts across the healthcare industry will be large. Traditionally, health practitioners are well-trained technically, and possess empathy and balanced interpersonal skills. It has been suggested that an increased use of technology will de-humanise medicine as practitioner-patient relationships become remote monitoring of data, and short meetings via webcam. There would be no physical interaction required and a reduction in trust between patients and doctors. Further to this, decisions are taken out of the hands of qualified medical experts, are made based on hypothetical success outcomes of data driven therapy strategies. This change could lead to significant job losses across the field. On the positive side, data science in health will vastly improve treatment and decision making. Automation of simple tasks will allow practitioners more time to improve patient relationships, and allow for better decision making as automation ensures access to large amounts of data. As seen already in the afore-mentioned Kaggle challenge, data science would lead to earlier detection, improved tracking and understanding of disease. The physical and mental monitoring and maintenance will be made easier. These impacts are true across all industries, in that data science will have both positive and negative effects.

    5. HYPER AUTOMATION

    What does it do?

    Hyper-automation is the combination of aspects such as intelligence, packaged software and automated tools. It is the combination of “automat[ed] technologies and artificial intelligence” to achieve the completion of task which were previously handled by man (Leapwork, 2020). This idea of expanding systems via the use of automation and AI is to perform tasks and fulfill objectives in a more efficient, quick and smooth process. Common place examples of hyper-automation in our daily lives are help desks and credit card applications. In both examples, the artificial intelligence in has been taught to understand the information which is relevant to leading to an outcome. In the case of the help desk, the computers in place are programmed to deal with incoming phone calls and can potentially provide a solution for a customer/client by having basic responses programmed into the AI. A customer may flag that they’re unhappy with a product they want to return a product, the AI can through a database or by means of code can direct the consumer to a solution delivering a pre-set response with instructions or may direct the consumer straight to an actual person if the matter was a more complex issue. In regards to the example of a credit card application, AI implemented to filter through them are now capable of collating all the necessary information such as credit reports and background information to decide whether an applicant is trustworthy enough to be sent a credit card. Hyper-Automation does not just stop at a maximising efficiency in simplistic tasks but also can be seen in large scale factories through the use of Robotic Process Automation. Many factories around the world now utilise robots of sorts which are programmed by humans to perform parts of an assembly line in order to fully construct machinery such as cars, laptops, phones and many other integral pieces of modern technology.

    What is the likely impact?

    The expansion of automation will inevitably put people out of work who previously performed the tasks which are now being occupied and carried out by AI technology and systems. However, the creation of the equipment and technology required to carry these tasks out has and will continue to also bring forth new employment opportunities which previously never existed. These new jobs required a high skill level as many of the programming and engineering of these products and systems need many years of tertiary education and previous experience within the fields of IT and engineering before a potential employee can be accepted into working within the field of Automated Technologies. For many businesses this will save large sums of funds which were previously allocated towards paying employees. These funds can now be redirected towards upkeep of machinery, in one example which I came across according to bernardmarr.com “a large… bank used 85 software bots to run 13 processes that handled 1.5 million requests in a year. … equalled 230 full-time employees… 30 per cent the cost of recruiting more staff.” (Bernard Marr, 2019). This one example is just one of many which can be seen across the world as more companies whether they be financially, computing or machine based this technology is here to stay and develop. Employment opportunities will rise for high skilled workers and corporations and business small or large will benefit financially but it may come at the cost of removing many thousands and potentially millions of jobs which previously existed.

    How will this affect you?

    In my own daily life, I will see and hear fewer human voices. At the moment I go to the shops and I use the self-service checkout, I pull cash out from an ATM, I order my lunch via a mobile application and if I want assistance regarding an issue with some software or a product the chances are I will be led to an FAQ website or hear a speech to text robot guiding me along the way to my desired solution. Those are examples of hyper-automation which are already in place. The expansion of hyper-automation is an inevitably of humans seeking to maximise the efficiency of tasks. At this current point in my life it mostly serves me positively and helps me to also maximise speed and efficiency of simple tasks on a daily basis. But I can’t help but feel the majority of jobs in the future will be replaced by robots programmed to power through tasks I can only a 1/10th or 1/100th of the speed. I have no doubt in my lifetime I will have family members and friends who work in factories or in IT for example who will replaced as a result of automation. It ultimately intends to serve us well, but potentially at the risk of losing human connection and interactions which previously were commonplace like talking to a cashier or hearing the voice of a help desk worker.
